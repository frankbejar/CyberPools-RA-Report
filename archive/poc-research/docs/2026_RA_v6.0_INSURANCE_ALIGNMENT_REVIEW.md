## Executive Summary

### Overall Assessment: STRONG with CRITICAL GAPS

The 2026 Risk Assessment v6.0 demonstrates strong alignment with insurance carrier requirements and has made significant improvements over v5.0 by restoring foundational CIS IG1 controls. However, the review identified **7 Critical findings** and **12 High Priority findings** that require immediate attention before the 2026 assessment cycle launch.

**Key Strengths:**
- TIER 1A controls (9 questions) accurately reflect universal carrier requirements (95%+ prevalence)
- MFA coverage is comprehensive (4 questions covering remote access, cloud, admin, critical systems)
- EDR/MDR requirement correctly positioned as TIER 1A with no antivirus fallback
- Backup protection requirements align with 2025 ransomware attack patterns
- Grade Ceiling methodology accurately predicts insurance outcomes

**Critical Issues Identified:**
1. PAM (Q3.5) classified as TIER 1B should be TIER 1A based on 2025 carrier data (42% required, up from 36%)
2. Network segmentation missing from TIER framework despite 70% carrier requirement
3. Endpoint encryption (Q3.7) classified as TIER 1B should be TIER 1A for healthcare/HIPAA organizations
4. Phishing simulation testing frequency not specified (quarterly vs annual matters for carrier compliance)
5. Missing explicit question about account lockout policies (credential stuffing attack vector)
6. Missing explicit question about secure RDP configuration (58% ransomware claims start from VPN/firewall compromise)
7. Vulnerability scanning question does not address internal scanning (only external)

### Insurance Outcome Prediction Accuracy

**Current State:** Grade Ceiling methodology correctly predicts insurance outcomes for TIER 1A gaps but may understate severity for TIER 1B gaps that are actually emerging TIER 1A requirements.

**Example Scenario:**
- Organization missing network segmentation (currently Comprehensive tier)
- Assessment Grade: A (no ceiling applied)
- Actual Insurance Outcome: 70% of carriers require segmentation, likely restrictions or premium surcharge of 15-25%
- **Verdict:** Assessment suggests excellent insurability, reality shows moderate restrictions

#### CRITICAL-1: PAM (Q3.5) Tier Misalignment

**Finding:** Privileged Access Management (Q3.5) is classified as TIER 1B but carrier data indicates it should be TIER 1A.

**Carrier Evidence:**
- **2025 Data:** 42% of organizations reported that PAM was required to obtain cyber insurance policy, up from 36% in 2023 (BeyondTrust, 2025)
- **Trend:** This represents a 6-percentage-point increase in just 2 years, indicating rapid shift toward mandatory status
- **Forrester Research:** 80% of security breaches involve privileged credentials (keys, certificates, passwords, tokens)
- **Current Classification:** TIER 1B (60% prevalence cited in document)
- **Actual Prevalence:** 42% required (mandatory), additional carriers use for rate optimization

**Claims Impact:**
- Admin account compromise leads to lateral movement in 89% of attacks (Verizon DBIR 2024)
- Hamilton City case: Compromised credentials led to $18.4M total recovery costs
- PAM prevents credential-based lateral movement that enables ransomware encryption of entire networks

**Insurance Reality:**
While 42% prevalence does not technically meet the "95%+ universal" TIER 1A threshold, the control is fundamentally different from other TIER 1B controls:
- **Rising rapidly:** 6-point increase in 2 years suggests 60-70% prevalence by 2026-2027
- **Foundational defense:** Prevents lateral movement that makes ransomware catastrophic vs contained
- **Claims differentiation:** Organizations with PAM contain breaches 54 days faster (IBM 2024)
- **Emerging gate:** Some carriers now require PAM for preferred rates, others for eligibility

**Assessment Impact:**
- Organization without PAM can achieve Grade A if all TIER 1A controls present
- Grade Ceiling does not apply (PAM is TIER 1B, ceiling only for missing TIER 1B = Grade B max)
- Board sees "Grade A" despite missing control that 42% of carriers require
- **Current:** Grade A (100% Foundation, 97% Comprehensive) = "Excellent insurability"
- **Reality:** Restrictions from 42% of carriers, premium surcharge likely

**Recommendation:**
**ACTION REQUIRED:** Reclassify Q3.5 (Privileged Access Management) from TIER 1B to TIER 1A based on:
1. 42% mandatory requirement prevalence (highest among TIER 1B controls)
2. Rapid growth trajectory (36% to 42% in 2 years)
3. Foundational role in preventing lateral movement (89% of attacks)
4. Claims cost differentiation (54-day faster containment)

**Alternative:** If maintaining TIER 1B classification, create "TIER 1B-Premium" designation that applies Grade B ceiling (not Grade A) to distinguish from other TIER 1B controls. This ensures organizations without PAM receive Grade B maximum, signaling material insurance impact.

**Category:** CRITICAL - TIER misalignment creates false confidence about insurability

- **Premium Impact:** Some carriers offer premium reductions of 15-25% for organizations with proven microsegmentation capabilities
- **OT/Manufacturing:** Carriers applying coverage exclusions or premium surcharges for operations without demonstrated segmentation, monitoring, and incident response capabilities

**Assessment Coverage:**
- **Q4.6:** "Network segmentation" (Impact: 3, Comprehensive tier)
- **Classification:** Comprehensive (not TIER 1A or TIER 1B)
- **Implication:** Organization without network segmentation can achieve Grade A

**Claims Impact:**
- Network segmentation limits lateral movement during cyber attacks by isolating critical systems
- Prevents ransomware from spreading from initial compromise point to entire network
- Zero Trust Segmentation (ZTS) stops breach spread by shutting down pathways for lateral movement
- 44% of claims are rejected because businesses don't meet basic security thresholds (segmentation is one of them)

**Insurance Reality:**
- 70% prevalence exceeds TIER 1B threshold (50-95%)
- Should be classified as TIER 1B minimum
- Absence should trigger Grade B ceiling (missing TIER 1B control)
- Current classification allows Grade A despite 70% carrier requirement

**Recommendation:**
**ACTION REQUIRED:** Reclassify Q4.6 (Network Segmentation) from Comprehensive to TIER 1B based on:
1. 70% carrier requirement prevalence (exceeds TIER 1B 50-95% threshold)
2. Premium discount/surcharge impact (15-25% differential)
3. Lateral movement prevention (fundamental ransomware defense)
4. Rising carrier mandate trend (especially OT/manufacturing/critical infrastructure)

**Enhancement:** Consider increasing Impact Rating from 3 to 5 based on lateral movement prevention role in ransomware attacks.

**Category:** CRITICAL - 70% prevalence control missing from TIER framework

#### CRITICAL-3: Endpoint Encryption Sector-Specific Gap (Healthcare/HIPAA)

**Finding:** Endpoint encryption (Q3.7) classified as TIER 1B "emerging requirement" but is mandatory for healthcare/HIPAA organizations.

**Carrier Evidence:**
- **HIPAA Organizations:** Full-disk encryption is required by HIPAA breach notification rules
- **Healthcare Breaches:** Lost/stolen devices account for 16% of healthcare breaches (HHS Breach Portal)
- **Safe Harbor:** If device is encrypted, breach notification is not required under HIPAA (significant cost/reputation savings)
- **Built-in Tools:** BitLocker (Windows) and FileVault (Mac) are free and built-in, making this a low-cost control

**Current Classification:**
- **Q3.7:** "Endpoint device encryption (full-disk encryption on laptops/mobile devices)" - Impact: 5, TIER 1B
- **Evidence:** "HIPAA required, emerging carrier requirement"
- **Issue:** Document acknowledges HIPAA requirement but classifies as "emerging" for general population

**Sector-Specific Analysis:**

**Healthcare/HIPAA Organizations (Vitalant, religious healthcare facilities):**
- Missing endpoint encryption = HIPAA violation
- Lost laptop without encryption = mandatory breach notification to HHS and affected individuals
- Average breach notification cost: $245 per record (IBM 2024)
- 100-record laptop theft = $24,500 notification cost + reputational damage
- **Should be:** TIER 1A for healthcare sector

**K-12 Schools (Primary CyberPools membership):**
- Student data on unencrypted devices = FERPA implications
- Lost device = potential breach notification to parents
- Reputational damage in school communities
- **Current TIER 1B classification may be appropriate** for K-12

**Assessment Impact:**
- CyberPools serves healthcare organizations (Vitalant expansion)
- Healthcare org without endpoint encryption receives Grade B (missing TIER 1B control)
- **Actual Insurance Reality:** Uninsurable or severe restrictions due to HIPAA violation
- Grade B understates severity for healthcare sector

**Recommendation:**
**ACTION REQUIRED:** Implement sector-specific TIER classification for Q3.7:
1. **Healthcare/HIPAA Organizations:** Classify Q3.7 as TIER 1A (mandatory HIPAA requirement)
2. **K-12/General Organizations:** Maintain Q3.7 as TIER 1B (emerging requirement)
3. **Assessment Logic:** Apply appropriate ceiling based on organization sector
4. **Grade Ceiling Impact:** Healthcare org missing Q3.7 receives Grade C maximum (missing TIER 1A), not Grade B

**Alternative:** Create "Sector-Critical" designation that applies TIER 1A ceiling for specific organization types. Document must clearly explain this distinction in member communications.

**Category:** CRITICAL - Sector-specific mandatory requirement not reflected in grading

#### CRITICAL-5: Missing Control - Account Lockout Policy

**Finding:** Assessment does not include explicit question about account lockout policies, despite credential stuffing being a major attack vector.

**Carrier Evidence:**
- **Attack Vector:** 47% of breaches involve stolen credentials (Verizon DBIR 2024)
- **Credential Stuffing:** Automated attacks try thousands of username/password combinations
- **Lockout Policies:** Prevent brute force attacks by temporarily locking accounts after failed login attempts
- **MFA Relationship:** Account lockout provides defense-in-depth even when MFA is bypassed or not implemented for all systems

**Current Coverage:**
- **Q2.3:** "Password policy (length, complexity, changes)" - Impact: 3, Comprehensive
- **Issue:** Password policy question does not address lockout policies
- **Gap:** No question about failed login attempt thresholds, lockout duration, or automated blocking

**Claims Impact:**
- Credential stuffing attacks target accounts without lockout policies
- Automated tools can try millions of password combinations against accounts
- Account lockout slows attackers and triggers security alerts
- Organizations without lockout policies experience higher rates of credential-based breaches

**Insurance Reality:**
- Not a universal carrier requirement (not TIER 1A)
- Considered foundational defense-in-depth (likely TIER 1B or Comprehensive)
- CIS Control 6.2: "Establish and Maintain a Software Inventory" includes account lockout as part of secure configuration
- NIST 800-63B: Recommends account lockout or rate limiting for authentication attempts

**Recommendation:**
**ACTION REQUIRED:** Add new question for account lockout policies:

**Proposed Q2.11:** "Does the organization enforce account lockout policies that temporarily disable accounts after a defined number of failed login attempts (e.g., 5 failed attempts = 15-minute lockout)?"
- **Impact:** 5 (prevents credential stuffing, automated brute force attacks)
- **Tier:** Comprehensive (foundational defense-in-depth, not universal carrier requirement)
- **Evidence:** Prevents brute force attacks, recommended by NIST 800-63B, CIS Control 6.2
- **Sector:** Applicable to all organizations (K-12, healthcare, religious)

**Alternative:** Enhance Q2.3 (Password policy) to include lockout policies as part of comprehensive password management assessment. Less preferable because it combines two distinct controls.

**Category:** CRITICAL - Missing foundational control that prevents credential-based attacks

#### CRITICAL-6: Missing Control - Secure RDP Configuration

**Finding:** Assessment does not include explicit question about Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) security, despite RDP being a primary ransomware entry point.

**Carrier Evidence:**
- **Attack Vector:** 58% of ransomware claims start from VPN/firewall compromise (Coalition 2025)
- **RDP Attacks:** Exposed RDP is one of the most common initial access vectors for ransomware
- **Shodan Searches:** Attackers use Shodan to find publicly exposed RDP endpoints
- **Credential Attacks:** RDP targeted by brute force and credential stuffing when exposed to internet

**Current Coverage:**
- **Q2.3:** "MFA for remote access/VPN" - TIER 1A (covers VPN, does not explicitly address RDP)
- **Q4.1:** "Firewall deployment (perimeter + segmentation)" - Comprehensive
- **Gap:** No explicit question about RDP configuration, exposure, or restrictions

**Claims Impact:**
- Exposed RDP without MFA = direct path for ransomware attackers
- Many organizations expose RDP to internet for remote support/access
- RDP attacks often succeed because:
  1. RDP exposed directly to internet (not behind VPN)
  2. Weak passwords on RDP accounts
  3. No MFA on RDP authentication
  4. No IP whitelisting or geofencing

**Insurance Reality:**
- Carriers assess RDP exposure in underwriting applications
- Organizations with internet-exposed RDP face higher premiums or restrictions
- Best practice: RDP only accessible via VPN with MFA, or IP whitelisting
- CISA Warning: "Secure RDP" is one of the top mitigations for ransomware

**Recommendation:**
**ACTION REQUIRED:** Add new question for RDP security:

**Proposed Q4.15:** "If Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) is used, is it secured by one or more of the following: (1) Only accessible via VPN with MFA, (2) MFA required for RDP authentication, (3) IP whitelisting/geofencing, (4) Disabled or not exposed to internet?"
- **Impact:** 5 (prevents ransomware initial access, primary attack vector)
- **Tier:** TIER 1B (foundational defense, 58% ransomware attacks involve remote access compromise)
- **Evidence:** Coalition 2025 Cyber Threat Index (58% ransomware from VPN/firewall), CISA ransomware guidance
- **Assessment Logic:** Fully = One or more protections in place, Partial = RDP used but weak controls, Not = RDP exposed to internet without protection

**Alternative:** Enhance Q2.3 (MFA for remote access/VPN) to explicitly include RDP in the question text. Less preferable because it combines authentication (MFA) with configuration (RDP exposure/restrictions).

**Category:** CRITICAL - Missing control for primary ransomware attack vector (58% of claims)

#### CRITICAL-7: Vulnerability Scanning Scope Gap (Internal vs External)

**Finding:** Q4.5 asks about "External vulnerability scanning (quarterly)" but does not address internal vulnerability scanning.

**Carrier Evidence:**
- **External Scanning:** Universal carrier requirement, explicitly required by The Trust
- **Internal Scanning:** Identifies vulnerabilities on internal network that external scanning cannot detect
- **Attack Vector:** After initial compromise, attackers scan internal network for unpatched systems to exploit for lateral movement
- **Coverage Gap:** External scanning only identifies internet-facing vulnerabilities, misses internal infrastructure weaknesses

**Current Question:**
- **Q4.5:** "External vulnerability scanning (quarterly)" - Impact: 5, TIER 1A
- **Evidence:** "Identifies internet-facing risks before exploitation. The Trust requirement."
- **Issue:** Only addresses external scanning, no question about internal scanning

**Claims Impact:**
- Ransomware attackers who gain initial access via phishing, VPN compromise, or other means then pivot to internal network reconnaissance
- Unpatched internal systems (servers, workstations, network devices) become targets for lateral movement
- External scanning would not detect these internal vulnerabilities
- Organizations with external-only scanning have false confidence in vulnerability management

**Insurance Reality:**
- External scanning is universal requirement (TIER 1A correct)
- Internal scanning is emerging best practice (likely TIER 1B or Comprehensive)
- CIS Control 7.5: "Perform Automated Vulnerability Scans of Internal Enterprise Assets" (IG2)
- Carriers may ask about internal scanning in applications but not universally required

**CIS IG1 vs IG2 Classification:**
- **CIS 7.1:** Establish and Maintain a Vulnerability Management Process (IG1)
- **CIS 7.4:** Perform Automated Application Patch Management (IG1)
- **CIS 7.5:** Perform Automated Vulnerability Scans of Internal Enterprise Assets (IG2)
- **Implication:** Internal scanning is IG2, not IG1, so Comprehensive tier may be appropriate

**Recommendation:**
**ACTION REQUIRED:** Add new question for internal vulnerability scanning:

**Proposed Q4.16:** "Does the organization perform internal vulnerability scanning of enterprise assets (servers, workstations, network devices) at least quarterly?"
- **Impact:** 5 (identifies internal vulnerabilities exploited during lateral movement)
- **Tier:** Comprehensive (CIS IG2, not universal carrier requirement but emerging best practice)
- **Evidence:** CIS Control 7.5 (IG2), prevents lateral movement exploitation of unpatched internal systems
- **Assessment Logic:** Fully = Quarterly or more frequent internal scanning, Partial = Annual or ad-hoc scanning, Not = No internal scanning

**Alternative:** Revise Q4.5 to include both external and internal scanning:
- **Current:** "External vulnerability scanning (quarterly)"
- **Revised:** "Vulnerability scanning (external quarterly minimum, internal quarterly preferred)"
- Less preferable because it combines two distinct control maturity levels (TIER 1A external + Comprehensive internal)

**Category:** CRITICAL - Scope gap creates false confidence in vulnerability management coverage

#### HIGH-2: Backup Testing Specificity (Q6.3)

**Finding:** Q6.3 asks about "Backup testing frequency" but does not specify full restoration testing vs. basic verification.

**Carrier Evidence:**
- **The Trust:** Requires backup testing (frequency and methodology matter)
- **Coalition:** Requires tested backups (full restoration preferred)
- **Industry Data:** 62% of untested backups fail at restoration (Rubrik 2024)
- **Insurance Impact:** Backup testing failures discovered during ransomware incidents result in claim complications

**Current Question:**
- **Q6.3:** "Backup testing frequency" - Impact: 5, TIER 1A
- **Evidence:** "62% untested backups fail at restoration [2d]. Backup failure = claim denial risk."
- **Issue:** Question does not distinguish between "verification testing" (checking that backup job completed) and "restoration testing" (actually restoring data and verifying usability)

**Assessment Ambiguity:**
- Organization that runs monthly backup verification reports answers "Fully Implemented"
- Organization that performs quarterly full restoration testing also answers "Fully Implemented"
- These are materially different practices with different failure rates

**Insurance Reality:**
- Carriers expect restoration testing, not just verification
- Verification testing proves backup ran, not that data is recoverable
- Restoration testing proves data can be recovered and is usable
- Some organizations discover backup failures only during ransomware incidents (when verification testing gave false confidence)

**Recommendation:**
**ACTION REQUIRED:** Revise Q6.3 to specify restoration testing:

**Option 1 (Preferred):** Clarify question wording
- **Current:** "Backup testing frequency"
- **Revised:** "Backup restoration testing frequency (quarterly minimum, verifying that backed-up data can be successfully restored and is usable)"

**Option 2:** Add assessment guidance
- Maintain current question
- Assessment guidance specifies: Fully = Quarterly restoration testing, Partial = Annual restoration or monthly verification only, Not = No testing
- Less preferable because ambiguity remains in question itself

**Category:** HIGH - Lack of specificity allows inadequate testing practices to receive full credit

#### HIGH-4: MFA Phishing-Resistant Requirement (Q2.3-2.6)

**Finding:** MFA questions do not distinguish between standard MFA (SMS, authenticator app) and phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2, hardware tokens, certificate-based).

**Carrier Evidence:**
- **CISA Guidance:** "Phishing-resistant MFA" is stronger and recommended for critical systems (CISA Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals)
- **Microsoft 2025:** "Phishing-resistant MFA blocks 99% of identity attacks" (Microsoft Digital Defense Report 2025)
- **Insurance Trend:** Some carriers now ask specifically about phishing-resistant MFA methods
- **2025 Reality:** Standard MFA (SMS, app) is baseline, phishing-resistant MFA is emerging premium requirement

**Current Questions:**
- **Q2.3-2.6:** "MFA for [remote access/cloud services/administrative accounts/critical systems]" - All TIER 1A
- **Evidence:** Cites phishing-resistant MFA benefits but does not require it in question
- **Issue:** Organization with SMS-based MFA answers "Fully Implemented" (meets baseline), organization with FIDO2 hardware keys also answers "Fully Implemented" (exceeds baseline)

**Assessment Ambiguity:**
- SMS-based MFA is vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks and phishing
- Authenticator app MFA is stronger but still vulnerable to sophisticated phishing (MFA fatigue attacks)
- FIDO2/hardware token MFA is phishing-resistant and represents gold standard
- Current questions do not distinguish between these materially different security postures

**Insurance Reality:**
- Standard MFA (SMS, app) meets current carrier baseline requirements
- Phishing-resistant MFA is emerging requirement for high-value or high-risk organizations
- Not yet universal requirement (not appropriate for TIER 1A mandatory classification)
- Likely appropriate for future differentiation in grading (premium vs. baseline)

**Recommendation:**
**MONITOR:** MFA method differentiation is not necessary for 2026 assessment cycle because:
1. Standard MFA meets current carrier baseline (95%+ prevalence)
2. Phishing-resistant MFA is emerging requirement, not universal
3. SMS-based MFA is significantly better than no MFA (blocking 99.9% of attacks per Microsoft)

**Action for 2027-2028 Assessment:** Consider adding phishing-resistant MFA assessment:

**Option A:** Add bonus/premium tier
- Maintain current Q2.3-2.6 as TIER 1A baseline (any MFA method)
- Add optional question: "Does organization use phishing-resistant MFA methods (FIDO2, hardware tokens, certificate-based) for administrative and critical system access?"
- Treat as "beyond baseline" for grade enhancement (A â†’ A+ consideration)

**Option B:** Specify in question
- Revise Q2.5 (MFA for administrative accounts): "MFA for administrative accounts (phishing-resistant methods preferred: FIDO2, hardware tokens, certificate-based)"
- Assessment Logic: Fully = Phishing-resistant MFA, Partial = Standard MFA (SMS, app), Not = No MFA
- Risk: May penalize organizations with strong standard MFA implementations

**Recommended Approach:** Monitor carrier requirement evolution. If phishing-resistant MFA reaches 50%+ prevalence by 2027, add as separate assessment dimension.
